Month-end close

A predictable close is the foundation: cutoff discipline, reconciliations, and an audit-ready close file.

Example: 10‑day close calendar

Adjust based on volume and team size.

  • D‑2 to D‑1: timesheets/expenses finalization + AR follow‑ups + cutoff communication
  • D+1: post all billing + clear unapplied cash + enter vendor bills received
  • D+2: payroll accrual (if needed) + WIP update (project firms)
  • D+3: bank/credit card reconciliations + AR/AP subledger tie‑outs
  • D+4: accruals + prepaids + depreciation + GST/HST reconciliation
  • D+5: draft financials + reasonableness check
  • D+6: variance commentary + project margin review
  • D+7: finalize, lock, archive close file + send reporting pack

Example: reconciliation working papers (minimum set)

A “close file” should let someone re-perform your logic.

  • Bank: statement PDF + reconciliation + reconciling items list (with expected clearance date)
  • AR: subledger-to-GL tie-out + aging + notes for 60/90+ items
  • AP: subledger-to-GL tie-out + unpaid bills report + cutoff review
  • Payroll: payroll register + accrual calc (if any) + remittance support
  • Taxes: GST/HST reconciliation + filing support (if filing)
  • Fixed assets: rollforward + depreciation schedule

Tip: keep one folder per month: “01 Bank”, “02 AR”, “03 AP”, “04 Accruals”, “05 Reporting”.

Example: accruals & cutoff checklist

Use this to stop “surprise expenses” and timing distortions.

  • Confirm last vendor bill date received; identify late bills expected (AP inbox + key vendors)
  • Accrue recurring items: utilities, professional fees, subcontractors, interest, payroll
  • Review large payments after month-end: do any belong in the prior month?
  • Review large invoices issued after month-end: are they prior-month work (WIP) or current-month?
  • Ensure prepaids schedule updated (new annual invoices) and amortization posted

Example: accrual memo (mini-template)

Keep one memo per judgement area. Short is fine.

  • Item: (e.g., subcontractor accrual)
  • Facts: what was received/delivered, dates, contract terms
  • Estimate basis: hours × rate / % complete / vendor confirmation
  • Entry: DR Expense (or WIP) / CR Accrued liabilities
  • Reversal plan: reverse next month or clear to actual invoice
  • Evidence: emails, contract, calculation sheet

Example: variance commentary (copy/paste)

Use the same structure every month.

  • Revenue: ↑/↓ vs last month due to ______ (volume / rate / timing). Key clients: A, B.
  • Gross margin: ↑/↓ due to ______ (utilization / subcontractors / realization).
  • Operating expenses: material variances: ______ (one-time vs recurring).
  • Cash: ending cash $____; drivers: collections $____, payroll $____, major payments $____.
  • Risks: top AR 60/90+ items; upcoming tax/payment dates; staffing/capacity constraints.
  • Actions: 3 actions for next month (e.g., bill WIP weekly; collect invoice #; reduce spend).

Project accounting

WIP and billing discipline protect margins and smooth cash flow. These examples assume a project-based service firm.

Example: WIP calculation (simple)

Time & materials (T&M) with approved timesheets.

  • Hours approved this month: 120
  • Blended billable rate: $200
  • Earned value: 120 × 200 = $24,000
  • Invoices issued this month: $15,000
  • WIP (unbilled): 24,000 − 15,000 = $9,000

If you don’t track WIP, you often under-bill and misread margin performance.

Example: weekly “bill WIP” routine (15 minutes)

  • Open WIP report (top 10 projects by unbilled)
  • Decision per project: Invoice now / hold (needs approval) / write-down (document)
  • Confirm any client constraints (PO limits, milestone gates)
  • Assign action + due date (who issues invoice, who follows up)

Weekly billing beats monthly billing for cash and dispute management.

Example: realization & margin integrity

Keep margin conversations factual.

  • Billable hours: 120 @ $200 = $24,000
  • Write-down: $2,000 (client scope constraint)
  • Net billable: $22,000 → Realization: 22,000 / 24,000 = 91.7%
  • Action: document the “why” and prevent repeat (scope, estimating, approvals)

Example: project health dashboard (minimum fields)

  • Budget / revised budget
  • Billed-to-date, WIP, AR outstanding
  • Hours-to-date vs budget, utilization
  • Realization % and key drivers of write-downs
  • Next billing date / milestone / PO limit

Example: timesheet → invoice workflow

  1. Timesheets submitted (weekly deadline)
  2. PM approval (locks billable status + notes for non-billable)
  3. Rate mapping (role/rate table; special client rates documented)
  4. Invoice draft generated from approved hours + expenses
  5. Controller review (cutoff, WIP, narrative, tax treatment)
  6. Issue invoice + attach support where required
  7. Collections log starts at invoice date (no waiting until past due)

Controls & policy

Controls should be simple, repeatable, and documented. Below are policy examples you can adapt.

Example: capitalization threshold

Policy statement (example):

Expenditures over $2,500 that provide benefits beyond one year are capitalized unless immaterial by nature. All capital additions require approval by ______ and must include supporting invoice and business purpose.

Example: prepaid expense schedule

Minimum fields:

  • Vendor
  • Service period (start/end)
  • Total paid
  • Monthly amortization
  • GL account
  • Link to invoice/contract

Rule: every new annual invoice gets added to the schedule the same day it’s entered.

Example: accounting memo outline

Use this for any judgement area:

  • Issue / question
  • Facts and assumptions
  • Applicable guidance / policy
  • Conclusion
  • Journal entry and timing
  • Evidence retained

Example: approval matrix (lightweight)

  • Under $500: team lead approval
  • $500–$2,500: controller approval
  • Over $2,500: owner/principal approval + capital/prepaid assessment

Example: documentation rule (simple)

  • If it impacts financial statements, keep a working paper
  • Every reconciling item needs an owner + expected clear date
  • Any write-down needs a reason + approval

Bonus: 13‑week cash flow (how to interpret)

The goal is early warning — not perfection. Review it weekly with the same rhythm.

Example: one-week forecast (cash basis)

  • Beginning cash: $100,000
  • Expected collections: $55,000 (from AR follow-ups)
  • Payroll (net): ($48,000)
  • Vendor payments: ($20,000)
  • Tax remittance: ($8,000)
  • Ending cash: $79,000

Example: actions when a “crunch week” appears

  • Pull collections forward (top 10 invoices, partial payments, deposits)
  • Push discretionary spend (travel, non-urgent vendor payments)
  • Bill WIP earlier (weekly vs monthly)
  • Set a minimum cash buffer and enforce it
